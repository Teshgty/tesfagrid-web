The Tesfa Grid: A Deterministic Harmonic Sieve and Its Structural Interactions with Prime Numbers

The foundational volume of the research programme. Introduces and rigorously formalizes the Tesfa Grid construction and establishes nine proved theorems across two parts.

Tesfaye Dereje Independent Researcher 33 pages Submitted for peer review
Full PDF → Open Problems

Abstract

We introduce and rigorously formalize the Tesfa Grid, a family of deterministic two-dimensional matrix operators defined by a six-row, variable-column construction and an immutable two-phase filling rule. This volume establishes nine proved theorems: the Column Closure Law; Upper and Lower Diagonal Mean Laws; the Row-6 Structural Prime Exclusion Theorem; the General Summation Formula; Prime-Only Block Divergence (R² = 0.9992); the Mod-6 Gap Constraint; the Twin Prime Hex-Spacing Theorem; and the Harmonic Sieve Theorem.

The volume further documents the Tesfa Wave, the Tesfa Gap Code, connections to the Riemann zeta function, and a spectral analysis of the prime residual sequence. Monte Carlo experiments yield a low-frequency power ratio of 0.406 versus 0.094 ± 0.018 for random permutations (Z ≈ 17σ). Sinusoidal fits of detrended column sums yield R² = 0.8622 at C = 600 with permutation p = 0.0000 in 5,000 trials. Cross-cycle residual correlation of 0.713 for real primes versus −0.184 for random-gap sequences establishes a structural signal unique to the prime distribution.

The Tesfa-Zeta Conjecture — that the scale-invariant prime envelope captured by the Tesfa Grid reflects a structural property of the prime sequence whose connection to the Riemann zeta function remains an open question — is formally stated. No claims are made regarding proof of the Riemann Hypothesis.

The nine theorems

  1. I
    Column Closure Law For every column j and any parameters a, d: g(1,j) + g(2,j) + g(3,j) = g(6,j) + 2(d − a). An exact arithmetic identity linking the upper block to the bottom row.
  2. II
    Upper Diagonal Mean Law [g(1, j−1) + g(3, j+1)] / 2 = g(2, j). The center of any diagonal triple in the upper block is the exact arithmetic mean of its endpoints.
  3. III
    Lower Diagonal Mean Law [g(6, j) + g(5, j+1) + g(4, j+2)] / 3 = g(5, j+1). The symmetric invariance in the lower block, under the faster 3:1 velocity of Phase II.
  4. IV
    Row-6 Structural Prime Exclusion In the canonical grid, row 6 entries are g(6, j) = 3(C + j). Every entry is divisible by 3 and greater than 3, hence always composite. A zero-cost geometric prime sieve that eliminates 1/6 of all candidates purely by construction.
  5. V
    General Summation Formula The column sum is S_j = 6A + (12C + 12j − 9)·D. The linear coefficient in j is 12D — the velocity-doubling signature of the two-phase rule, independent of C.
  6. VI
    Prime-Only Block Divergence In prime-only mode, the block divergence (lower − upper column sums) grows linearly: Δ_j ≈ 52.1 j + const, with R² = 0.9992 and p < 4.4×10⁻¹²². A new two-dimensional prime gap metric.
  7. VII
    Mod-6 Gap Constraint For all prime gaps g = p_{n+1} − p_n with p_n > 3: g ≡ 0, 2, or 4 (mod 6). No prime gap (after the initial gap of 1) is congruent to 1, 3, or 5 modulo 6.
  8. VIII
    Twin Prime Hex-Spacing For any two consecutive twin prime pairs (p, p+2) and (q, q+2) with p, q > 3, the gap q − p is divisible by 6.
  9. IX
    Harmonic Sieve Theorem The generalized Tesfa Grid operator T_{C,w} has exact spectral nulls at frequencies f_k = k/C for k = 1, …, w−1. The transfer function H(f) satisfies H(f_k) = 0 at every null, proved via the Dirichlet kernel. The operator preserves the DC component while annihilating exactly w−1 oscillatory frequencies per period.

Structure of the volume

Part I — The Integer Grid: Construction, Theorems, and Prime Interactions
Construction and canonical example · Five structural theorems (Theorems I – V) · Prime-marked mode · Row-wise density and the Tesfa Wave · Prime-only mode and Block Divergence (Theorem VI) · Deep statistical analysis · The Tesfa Gap Code (Theorems VII, VIII) · Perfect numbers, zeta connections, twin primes.
Part II — The Harmonic Sieve: Spectral Analysis and Computational Experiments
The generalized operator T_{C,w} · The Harmonic Sieve Theorem (Theorem IX) and its proof via Dirichlet kernels · Monte Carlo significance experiments · The Tesfa Wave as a sinusoidal structure in prime residuals · Cross-cycle geometric resonance · Resonance width analysis · Deep sequence prediction.
Part III — Unified Discussion, Open Problems, and Conclusion
Methodology, interpretation, and limitations · Unified open problems · Conclusion.
Note on scope

All results in Part II are empirical. The Harmonic Sieve Theorem is proved, but the claim that prime-indexed inputs exhibit enhanced low-frequency ratios is a conjecture supported by numerical evidence, not a theorem. The Tesfa-Zeta Conjecture remains open.
