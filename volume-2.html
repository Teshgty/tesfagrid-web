Volume II · Preprint · March 2026 · Headline result

Empirical Evidence for the Tesfa-Zeta Conjecture

Three independent spectral methods consistently identify peaks in the Tesfa Grid residual spectrum at frequencies coinciding with the imaginary parts of the non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function. Sub-0.1 accuracy on multiple zeros. All signals absent in shuffled, noise, and fake-prime controls.

Tesfaye Dereje March 2026 14 pages Preprint — Not a proof of the Riemann Hypothesis
Full PDF → Read Volume I

Abstract

We present the first direct experimental test of the Tesfa-Zeta Conjecture — the proposition that the Fourier transform of the Tesfa Grid residuals ε(j) = ρ(j) − 1/ln(6j), computed in logarithmic column-index space, produces spectral peaks at frequencies coinciding with the imaginary parts γ_n of the non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function.

Three independent methods were applied across grid widths C = 500, 1,000, 2,000, 5,000, and 10,000, with 300 randomized controls per C value. The Lomb-Scargle analysis identifies peaks aligning with known zeta zeros at sub-0.1 accuracy, including δ = 0.012 for γ₁ = 14.135 at C = 2,000 and δ = 0.019 for γ₁₅ = 65.113 at C = 5,000. The matched-filter test finds 10 of 30 zeta zeros with z-score above 2.0 at C = 10,000, against 1.5 expected by chance (p < 0.001).

All signals are absent in shuffled-residual, white-noise, and fake-prime controls. These results constitute strong empirical support for the Tesfa-Zeta Conjecture. The Riemann Hypothesis remains unproved.

Headline result

δ = 0.012 for γ₁ = 14.135 at C = 2,000 (0.085% accuracy). δ = 0.019 for γ₁₅ = 65.113 at C = 5,000 (0.029% accuracy). δ = 0.030 for γ₄ = 30.425 at C = 10,000 (0.098% accuracy). Three sub-0.03 matches at three different grid widths and three different zeros, each confirmed by an independent matched-filter z-score exceeding 2.0.

Three independent methods, converging

Each method was applied at five grid widths with 300-trial randomization controls. All three methods agree on the central finding.

Method 1 — Log-space DFT
2+ exact matches
Direct discrete Fourier transform with ln(6j) as time variable. Peaks within δ < 2 of known zeros at every C.
Method 2 — Lomb-Scargle
δ < 0.03
Optimal for logarithmically-sampled data. Six tight matches across C values. Sharpest: γ₁₅ at 0.029% accuracy.
Method 3 — Matched Filter
10 / 30 significant
z-score versus 300 shuffled controls. Expected by chance: 1.5. Observed at C = 10,000: 10. p < 0.001.
Control specificity
Zero
Shuffled residuals, white noise, and fake-prime sequences all fail to reproduce the signal. Unique to the real prime sequence.

What this establishes

The Tesfa Grid residuals, analyzed in log(j) frequency space, carry power elevated above random baseline at frequencies coinciding with the imaginary parts of Riemann zeta zeros. This is confirmed by three independent methods at five grid sizes with 300-trial randomization controls.

What this does not establish

This result does not prove the Riemann Hypothesis. It does not prove the Tesfa-Zeta Conjecture (which requires convergence as C → ∞). The analytic connection between the Tesfa Grid operator and the zeta function remains open.

Correct statement

The Tesfa-Zeta Conjecture has passed its first direct experimental test. The evidence is strong enough to report. It is not strong enough to claim proof of anything beyond what the data directly shows.

Open problems

OP 1TZC Convergence

Does δ_n(C) → 0 as C → ∞ for all n? Prove or disprove that Lomb-Scargle peak positions converge to the imaginary parts γ_n of the zeta zeros. Empirical fit at current scale: δ_n(C) ~ C^(−α) with α ≈ 0.12.

OP 2Null-to-Gap Alignment

Prove that the Tesfa Harmonic Sieve nulls at f_k = k/C are positioned between consecutive γ_n values, making the Tesfa operator a natural band-pass filter for zeta-zero frequencies.

OP 3Amplitude Formula

Derive an analytic expression for the z-score z(γ_n) as a function of C, γ_n, and the Tesfa operator transfer function. If z(γ_n) → ∞ as C → ∞, this implies convergence.

OP 4Critical Line

Can the Tesfa spectral framework constrain the real part of the zeta zeros to Re(ρ) = 1/2? This would constitute a new approach to the Riemann Hypothesis — one not rooted in contour integration or random matrix methods, but in discrete geometric sieving.
