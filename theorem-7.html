Theorem 7

A mod-6 constraint on consecutive prime gaps

Abstract

We state a constraint on consecutive prime gaps arising from the residue structure of primes greater than three. The constraint eliminates half of the a-priori possible gap residues modulo six and has been verified empirically across 348,511 consecutive prime gaps with zero violations. The proof is elementary and uses only classical facts about prime residues. The theorem's application to computational prime generation is pursued separately; this page states the theorem and records its empirical confirmation.

§1. Statement

Theorem 7

Let pk and pk+1 be consecutive primes with pk > 3. Define the prime gap

gk = pk+1 − pk.

Then

gk ≡ 0, 2, or 4 (mod 6).

Equivalently, no prime gap between primes greater than 3 is congruent to 1, 3, or 5 modulo 6.

§2. Proof outline

The theorem follows from a single classical observation: every prime p > 3 is congruent to either 1 or 5 modulo 6. Any other residue modulo 6 is either divisible by 2 or by 3, excluding primality. The difference of two such primes therefore falls into one of four cases (1 → 1, 1 → 5, 5 → 1, 5 → 5), each of which produces a gap congruent to 0, 2, or 4 modulo 6. The full proof is short and entirely elementary.

Readers wishing to reproduce the proof in detail are invited to derive it directly from the above observation, or to write to research@ethoryx.io for the complete derivation used in our applied work.

§3. Empirical verification

Though the proof is complete, empirical verification confirms the theorem at scale and gives the distribution of gap residues in practice. We computed all consecutive prime gaps for primes up to 5 × 106, yielding 348,511 gaps, and recorded each gap's residue modulo 6.

Zero violations across the full sample. The approximate symmetry between residues 2 and 4 reflects the equal frequency of the two cross-residue prime transitions.

§4. Context and onward work

Theorem 7 is elementary; similar statements appear implicitly in any treatment of the distribution of primes modulo small moduli. What we offer here is a precise statement, a careful empirical confirmation at meaningful scale, and the identification of this result as a foundation for onward work.

Several further results within the Tesfa Grid programme depend on this theorem. Volume II (the Column-Class Sieve) presents one such application, a prime-generation method that exploits the residue structure identified here. Volume III (the Oscillation Law) describes an empirical regularity in gap second-differences whose interpretation is cleaner when the constraint of Theorem 7 is assumed. Details of the applied constructions, including the specific parameter choices and layer integrations used in the Ethoryx commercial implementation, are treated as engineering matters and are not described in full on this site.
