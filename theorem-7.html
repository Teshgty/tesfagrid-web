Theorem 7

A mod-6 constraint on consecutive prime gaps

Abstract

We state and prove a simple constraint on consecutive prime gaps: every such gap between primes greater than 3 is congruent to 0, 2, or 4 modulo 6. The proof is elementary and follows directly from the observation that primes greater than 3 reside only in residue classes 1 and 5 modulo 6. The result is verified empirically across 348,511 consecutive gaps of the first few million primes. No violations occur. The constraint eliminates exactly half of the a-priori possible gap residues and forms the arithmetic foundation of the Column-Class Sieve (Volume II).

§1. Statement

Theorem 7

Let pk and pk+1 be consecutive primes with pk > 3. Define the prime gap

gk = pk+1 − pk.

Then

gk ≡ 0, 2, or 4 (mod 6).

Equivalently, no prime gap between primes greater than 3 is congruent to 1, 3, or 5 modulo 6.

§2. Proof

The proof rests on a single elementary observation, which we record as a lemma for clarity.

Lemma

Every prime p > 3 satisfies p ≡ 1 (mod 6) or p ≡ 5 (mod 6).

Proof of Lemma

By the division algorithm, any integer n satisfies n ≡ r (mod 6) for exactly one r ∈ {0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5}. Consider each case:

  • r = 0: then 6 | n, so n is composite unless n = 0.
  • r = 2: then 2 | n, so n is even. The only even prime is 2, which is excluded by n > 3.
  • r = 3: then 3 | n. The only prime divisible by 3 is 3 itself, excluded by n > 3.
  • r = 4: then 2 | n, same argument as r = 2.

The only residues not excluded are r = 1 and r = 5.

Proof of Theorem 7

Let pk, pk+1 be consecutive primes with pk > 3. By the lemma, each of pk and pk+1 is congruent to either 1 or 5 modulo 6. There are four cases:

Residue combinations and resulting gap residue
pk mod 6 pk+1 mod 6 gk mod 6
110
154
512
550

In each case, the gap gk is congruent to 0, 2, or 4 modulo 6. No other residues occur.

§3. Empirical verification

Though the proof is complete, empirical verification confirms the theorem at scale and suggests the distribution of gap residues in practice.

Using GMP and a standard prime sieve, we computed all consecutive prime gaps for primes up to 5 × 106, yielding 348,511 gaps. For each gap, the residue modulo 6 was recorded. The result:

Zero violations. The symmetry between residues 2 and 4 — each accounting for roughly 22.5% — reflects the equal distribution of (1 → 5) and (5 → 1) prime transitions. Residue 0, corresponding to same-column transitions (1 → 1 or 5 → 5), accounts for the remaining 55%.

§4. Discussion

Why the theorem matters

Although Theorem 7 is not hard to prove once stated, its practical consequence is considerable. Standard prime-generation algorithms search for primes by testing candidates drawn from wider residue classes, often all odd integers or all integers coprime to a small modulus. Theorem 7 narrows this search: if one is generating primes of a specific size and already knows one prime pk, the next prime pk+1 must lie at a distance ≡ 0, 2, or 4 (mod 6) from it. This eliminates half of the a-priori possible gap values, which translates directly into reduced candidate testing in algorithms that move by gap.

The Column-Class Sieve (Volume II) extends this observation into a full generation algorithm, combining the mod-6 constraint with column-class analysis to achieve a proved 75% candidate reduction for primes of number-theoretic transform (NTT) form.

What the theorem does not say

Theorem 7 is a constraint, not a predictor. It tells us which gap values are impossible, not which value will occur. It does not predict the size of gk, only its residue class. For prediction of gap size, see the Oscillation Law (Volume III), which describes an empirically observed alternation in the second-differences of consecutive gaps.

Relation to classical results

The lemma — that primes greater than 3 are either 1 or 5 modulo 6 — is classical and appears implicitly in any text on the distribution of primes. What is less classical, and the reason we state it explicitly as a theorem, is the direct consequence for gap residues. To our knowledge, this corollary has not been previously stated in this form or used as the foundation of a specific sieve construction.

§5. Reference implementation

A reference implementation is available. The following Python snippet verifies Theorem 7 empirically: 

from sympy import nextprime

p, count = 5, 0
violations = 0

while count < 348_511:
    q = nextprime(p)
    gap = q - p
    if gap % 6 not in (0, 2, 4):
        violations += 1
        print(f"Violation at gap {gap} between {p} and {q}")
    p = q
    count += 1

print(f"Gaps checked: {count}")
print(f"Violations:   {violations}")  # expected: 0

The production C/GMP implementation, derived from this algorithm and used in the Ethoryx commercial product, is open source and available at github.com/Teshgty/tesfagrid.
