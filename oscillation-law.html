The Oscillation Law

An empirical regularity in prime-gap second-differences

Abstract

We report an empirical observation concerning the second-differences of consecutive prime gaps. Across a sample of 348,511 consecutive gaps, the sign of the second-difference alternates at a frequency meaningfully greater than that expected under independent random gaps. The excess is large and statistically unambiguous.

We record the observation as a conjecture. No proof is offered and we do not claim one. Heuristic interpretations exist but are not presented here.

§1. Statement

Definition · second-difference of gaps

For consecutive primes pk, pk+1, pk+2, define the gaps gk = pk+1 − pk and gk+1 = pk+2 − pk+1, and the second-difference

G2(k) = gk+1 − gk.

The sign sgn G2(k) ∈ {−1, 0, +1} records whether the next gap is larger, equal to, or smaller than the current gap.

Conjecture · the Oscillation Law

For consecutive primes greater than 3, the sign of G2(k) alternates more often than chance. The proportion of indices k for which sgn G2(k) · sgn G2(k+1) = −1 tends to a limit strictly greater than 1/2.

The law is stated as a conjecture because it has not been proved. The empirical evidence is nonetheless substantial.

§2. Empirical evidence

Using the first 5 × 106 primes, we computed the sequence of second-differences and tabulated the frequency of sign alternations between consecutive entries.

Statistical significance

Under the null hypothesis of independent random signs (ignoring zero entries), the expected alternation rate is 50%. The observed rate of 68.2% corresponds to a z-score of approximately 211, effectively ruling out the possibility that the observation is due to statistical noise in the sample.

§3. Status and interpretation

The observation is robust under the specific sampling range examined. Whether the alternation rate converges to a limit as k → ∞, and what that limit would be, are open questions. Whether the observed rate of 68.2% is a finite-sample artefact or approximates a genuine asymptotic constant is likewise open.

Heuristic arguments can be constructed to motivate why such an alternation might be expected; these arguments fall short of a proof. We do not present them here.

§4. Open questions

  • Does the alternation rate converge as the sample range grows, and if so to what value?
  • Does the pattern derive from known results on the distribution of primes?
  • Does a corresponding pattern exist in higher-order differences?
  • Do analogous patterns appear in restricted sequences such as twin primes or prime constellations?
