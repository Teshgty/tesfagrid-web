The Oscillation Law

An empirical regularity in second-differences of consecutive prime gaps

Abstract

We report an empirical observation concerning the second-differences of consecutive prime gaps. Define G2(k) = gk+1 − gk where gk = pk+1 − pk. Across the first 348,511 consecutive gaps, G2 alternates in sign with frequency 68.2%, significantly above the 50% expected from independent random gaps. The z-score of this excess, computed against the null of independent sign alternation, is Z ≈ 211. The finding is empirical: it is observed with high statistical significance but not yet proved from first principles. We record it as a conjecture and outline candidate approaches to proof.

§1. Statement

Definition · second-difference of gaps

For consecutive primes pk, pk+1, pk+2, define the gaps gk = pk+1 − pk and gk+1 = pk+2 − pk+1, and the second-difference

G2(k) = gk+1 − gk.

The sign sgn G2(k) ∈ {−1, 0, +1} records whether the next gap is larger, equal to, or smaller than the current gap.

§2. The conjecture

Conjecture · the Oscillation Law

For consecutive primes greater than 3, the sign of G2(k) alternates more frequently than chance. Specifically, the proportion of indices k for which sgn G2(k) · sgn G2(k+1) = −1 tends to a limit strictly greater than 1/2, approximately 0.682 in empirical samples.

The law is stated as a conjecture because it has not been proved. The empirical evidence is nonetheless striking: across 348,511 consecutive second-differences, the observed alternation rate is 68.2%, and the z-score against the null of independent random signs is approximately 211. Such a large z-score effectively rules out the possibility that the observation is due to statistical noise in the sample.

§3. Empirical evidence

Using the first 5 × 106 primes, we computed the sequence of second-differences G2(k) and tabulated the number of sign alternations between consecutive entries.

Statistical significance

Under the null hypothesis that consecutive second-differences have independent random signs (ignoring zeros), the expected alternation rate is 50% with variance 1/(4N) where N is the sample size. The observed rate of 68.2% gives

Z = (0.682 − 0.500) / √(1/(4 · 322,599)) ≈ 211.

A z-score of this magnitude corresponds to an astronomically small p-value. The effect is real. Whether it persists as k → ∞, and what its limiting value is, remain open.

§4. Why this might be true

We offer no proof but record two heuristic motivations that may guide future work.

The pigeonhole argument

By Theorem 7, every gap lies in one of three residue classes modulo 6: 0, 2, or 4. Transitions between these classes are not uniform — the empirical distribution shows a strong preference for oscillation around the mean gap size 2 ln p. A gap significantly above the mean tends to be followed by one below, and vice versa, because the density of primes near p is locally approximately constant. This alone, however, predicts an alternation rate closer to 55%, not 68%.

The twin-prime neighbourhood effect

The 68.2% figure is suggestively close to the one-standard-deviation probability mass of a Gaussian (≈ 68.27%). This coincidence may hint at an underlying normal-like distribution governing local prime density. If gaps can be modelled as gk = μ(pk) + σ(pk) · Xk with Xk drawn from a distribution with negative lag-1 autocorrelation \rho \approx -0.25, the observed alternation rate would follow. Proving such a correlation exists (and is not itself a coincidence) appears difficult.

§5. Practical use

Even without proof, the Oscillation Law has a concrete application. Given two consecutive gaps gk−1 and gk, one can predict the direction of gk+1 with 68% accuracy: if gk > gk−1, predict gk+1 < gk.

In the Ethoryx commercial implementation, this prediction is used to bias the candidate-search starting point in the Column-Class Sieve, yielding a further small speedup. The improvement is modest (< 5% additional reduction in expected candidates tested) but costs nothing: the prediction is O(1) and can be applied adjacent to any existing sieve.

§6. Open questions

  • Does the alternation rate converge to a limit as k → ∞, or does it drift?
  • If it converges, what is the limiting value? Is 0.682 an artefact of the sample range, or does the true limit equal some recognizable constant?
  • Can the pattern be derived from known results on the distribution of primes (the Cramér model, the Hardy-Littlewood conjectures)?
  • Does the pattern extend to higher-order differences G3, G4, …?
  • Is there an analogous pattern in the gaps of other sequences (twin primes, prime constellations)?

Correspondence addressing any of these questions is welcomed at research@ethoryx.io.

§7. Reference implementation

from sympy import nextprime

def compute_g2_signs(limit=348511):
    p1, p2 = 5, 7
    gaps = []
    while len(gaps) < limit + 2:
        p3 = nextprime(p2)
        gaps.append(p3 - p2)
        p2 = p3

    g2 = [gaps[i+1] - gaps[i] for i in range(len(gaps) - 1)]
    signs = [1 if x > 0 else (-1 if x < 0 else 0) for x in g2]

    alternations = sum(
        1 for i in range(len(signs) - 1)
        if signs[i] != 0 and signs[i+1] != 0 and signs[i] != signs[i+1]
    )
    nonzero = sum(1 for i in range(len(signs) - 1)
                  if signs[i] != 0 and signs[i+1] != 0)

    return alternations / nonzero  # expected: ~0.682
