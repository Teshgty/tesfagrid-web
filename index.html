A geometric framework
for the structure of primes

The Tesfa Grid is a matrix arrangement in which every prime greater than 3 occupies a restricted set of columns. What appears chaotic at the level of individual primes becomes more ordered at the level of rows and residues. This observation is the foundation of a research programme: a growing body of theorems, empirical findings, and applied constructions concerning the deep structure of number theory.

Tesfaye Dereje · Addis Ababa · 2026
Abstract

Prime numbers are traditionally studied as points on a number line. The Tesfa Grid recasts them as coordinates in a two-dimensional lattice indexed by their residues modulo six. In this lattice, certain regularities that are obscure on the integer line become more visible, and can be stated as theorems, verified empirically, or put to computational use.

This programme has so far produced three research notes. The first (Theorem 7) is a proved constraint on consecutive prime gaps, verified across 348,511 gaps with zero violations. The second (the Column-Class Sieve) is a prime-generation method derived from the first. The third (the Oscillation Law) is an empirical regularity governing second-differences of gaps, stated as a conjecture. Further volumes are in preparation.

I. Why a grid

The prime numbers refuse to cooperate with most attempts at prediction. Gauss, Riemann, Littlewood, and Erdős each advanced the theory by degrees but the primes remain, in Erdős's phrase, a subject about which mathematicians know little and suspect less. This programme does not claim to resolve what they could not. It claims something smaller and more concrete: that certain patterns, obscure on the integer line, become more apparent when primes are displayed on a grid indexed by their residues modulo six, and that these patterns can be stated, tested, and sometimes put to use.

The construction is elementary. Every integer n > 3 satisfies n ≡ r (mod 6) for exactly one r ∈ {0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5}. Since 6 = 2·3, any integer with residue 0, 2, 3, 4 is composite. Primes greater than 3 therefore occupy only columns r = 1 and r = 5. The Tesfa Grid arranges integers in this six-column layout and asks what becomes visible when primes are marked.

What the grid reveals

Three findings have been extracted from this construction so far, each with a different character. The first is a proved constraint on prime gaps. The second is a prime-generation method derived from it. The third is an empirical regularity in gap second-differences, currently stated as a conjecture.

Theorem 7 · a brief statement

Any two consecutive primes pk and pk+1 greater than 3 satisfy gk ≡ 0, 2, or 4 (mod 6) for the gap gk = pk+1 − pk. Verified empirically across 348,511 consecutive gaps with zero violations. Read the note →

III. What this is and what it is not

The Tesfa Grid is an independent research programme, conducted from Addis Ababa, Ethiopia. It is not affiliated with a university or funded by a grant. Its findings are offered openly, under the ordinary conventions of a research note: statements are made precisely, proof outlines are given where available, empirical evidence is quantified where proofs are not, and no claim exceeds what the work supports.

The programme has a commercial sibling at ethoryx.io, which produces cryptographic software derived from these results. The two are kept separate. What lives on this site is the mathematics. What lives on ethoryx.io is the engineering. Detailed parameter choices, specific implementation decisions, and source code for the applied constructions are held internally and are made available to collaborators and commercial licensees rather than posted publicly.

The reader

These pages are written for the working mathematician, the cryptographer who wants to understand what lies beneath the algorithm, and the serious student encountering this material for the first time. Where notation can be unpacked in a sentence, it is unpacked. Where the underlying machinery is deep, pointers are provided.
