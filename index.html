A geometric framework
for the structure of primes

The Tesfa Grid is a 6×C matrix in which every prime greater than 3 occupies exactly two columns. What appears chaotic at the level of individual primes becomes predictable at the level of rows and residues. This is the foundation of a proved theorem, a faster sieve, and a growing body of work on the deep structure of number theory.

Tesfaye Dereje · Addis Ababa · 2026
Abstract

Prime numbers are traditionally studied as points on a number line. The Tesfa Grid recasts them as coordinates in a two-dimensional lattice indexed by their residues modulo six. In this lattice, certain theorems that are opaque on the line become visible as geometric facts — proved, testable, and computationally useful.

This programme has produced one proved theorem (a gap constraint holding across 348,511 consecutive prime gaps with zero violations), one new algorithm (the Column-Class Sieve, which reduces candidate space by 75% for number-theoretic transform primes), and one empirical regularity (the Oscillation Law governing second-differences of gaps). Each finding has both mathematical and cryptographic consequence.

I. Why a grid

The prime numbers refuse to cooperate with most attempts at prediction. Gauss, Riemann, Littlewood, and Erdős each advanced the theory by degrees but the primes remain, in Erdős's phrase, a subject about which mathematicians know little and suspect less. This programme does not claim to resolve what they could not. It claims something smaller and more concrete: that certain patterns, invisible on the integer line, become plain when primes are displayed on a grid indexed by their residues modulo six.

The construction is elementary. Every integer n > 3 satisfies exactly one of the congruences n ≡ r (mod 6) for r ∈ {0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5}. Since 6 = 2·3, any integer with residue 0, 2, 3, 4 is composite. Primes greater than 3 therefore occupy only columns r = 1 and r = 5. The Tesfa Grid arranges integers in this six-column layout and asks what becomes visible when primes are marked.

What the grid reveals

Three findings have been extracted from this construction so far, each with a different character. The first is a proved theorem about the arithmetic structure of prime gaps. The second is an algorithm derived from that theorem, faster than the standard sieve when the target is a prime of special form. The third is an empirical law governing how gaps alternate — observed, not yet proved, but stable across large samples.

Theorem 7 · the mod-6 gap constraint

For any two consecutive primes pk and pk+1 both greater than 3, the gap gk = pk+1 − pk satisfies gk ≡ 0, 2, or 4 (mod 6). No gap is ever ≡ 1, 3, or 5 (mod 6).

Verified empirically across 348,511 consecutive prime gaps. Zero violations. Read the proof →

III. What this is and what it is not

The Tesfa Grid is an independent research programme, conducted from Addis Ababa, Ethiopia. It is not affiliated with a university or funded by a grant. Its findings are offered openly, under the ordinary conventions of mathematical publication: statements are made precisely, proofs are given where available, empirical evidence is quantified where proofs are not, and no claim exceeds what the work supports.

The programme has a commercial sibling at ethoryx.io, which produces cryptographic software derived from these results. The two are kept separate. What lives on this site is the mathematics. What lives on ethoryx.io is the engineering. They are intended to inform each other, not to be confused with each other.

The reader

These pages are written for the working mathematician, the cryptographer who wants to understand what lies beneath the algorithm, and the serious student encountering this material for the first time. Where notation can be unpacked in a sentence, it is unpacked. Where the underlying machinery is deep, references are provided and the reader is trusted to pursue them.
