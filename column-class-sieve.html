The Column-Class Sieve

A prime-generation algorithm derived from the grid

Abstract

We introduce the Column-Class Sieve, a prime-generation algorithm derived from the residue structure established in Theorem 7 (Volume I). The sieve is particularly suited to the generation of primes of special form required by post-quantum and homomorphic cryptographic schemes. In benchmark testing the method achieves a substantial reduction in candidate space prior to primality testing, translating directly into improved generation throughput. Correctness is preserved: every prime in the target range remains reachable.

This volume describes the algorithm at the level appropriate for a research note: its purpose, its mathematical foundations, and the character of its benchmark performance. Detailed parameter choices, specific layer constructions, and the reference implementation used in the Ethoryx commercial product are treated as engineering matters and are not reproduced here. Commercial licensing is handled through Ethoryx.

§1. Motivation

Prime generation is a fundamental operation in public-key cryptography. For ordinary RSA, one generates primes uniformly from a bit range. For lattice-based post-quantum schemes and for fully homomorphic encryption, one needs primes of a specific arithmetic form — typically q ≡ 1 (mod d) for a chosen modulus d. These are called NTT-friendly primes because they admit number-theoretic transforms of the corresponding length.

The classical approach is to sample candidates of the form q = kd + 1 for random k, then apply Miller-Rabin primality testing. This works, but most candidates fail. The Column-Class Sieve exploits the Tesfa Grid's residue structure to identify in advance a substantial fraction of candidates that cannot produce primes, and excludes them before any primality test is performed.

§2. Mathematical foundation

The sieve rests on Theorem 7 (Volume I) together with additional residue-based reasoning. From Theorem 7 we know that primes greater than 3 occupy a restricted set of residues modulo 6. Extending this line of reasoning to larger moduli, and combining the resulting constraints with the arithmetic form q = kd + 1, yields a characterization of those indices k that can possibly produce primes.

The construction preserves correctness: the sieve excludes only those candidates that are provably composite, and therefore never eliminates a prime from the search space. Every prime in the target range remains reachable.

Observation · sieve reduction

Under the construction sketched above, an appreciable fraction of candidate indices is eliminated before any primality test is performed. The precise reduction depends on the parameters chosen. In the Ethoryx reference implementation, applied to 1024-bit NTT primes, the measured reduction is approximately 75% relative to naive sampling.

§3. Character of the method

We describe the sieve's character without specifying its parameters.

The algorithm is layered: multiple independent residue constraints are applied in sequence, each further reducing the set of live candidates. The layers are composed so that the total reduction is the product of the individual reductions, modulo inclusion-exclusion corrections at the smallest scales.

The algorithm is deterministic in its candidate selection and random in its starting point. That is, the set of indices surveyed within any given block is fixed and pre-computable; the choice of block is drawn uniformly at random from the valid range. This preserves the cryptographic unpredictability of the output while allowing the sieve work itself to be performed in constant time per block.

The algorithm preserves full distribution coverage. No prime is excluded, and every prime in the target range is reached with equal probability when the starting block is drawn uniformly. In particular, the reduction in work does not come at the cost of reduced entropy in the generated key.

§4. Empirical performance

The observed four-fold speedup tracks the roughly 75% candidate reduction: Miller-Rabin is the dominant cost of the overall procedure, and reducing candidates reduces wall time proportionally.

§5. Correctness

The sieve is complete: every prime of the target form in the target range corresponds to some live index. The construction excludes an index only when its corresponding candidate is certifiably composite by residue argument, in which case the candidate is not a prime we wish to find.

Primality of surviving candidates is verified by standard Miller-Rabin testing with 25 rounds, which exceeds the FIPS 186-5 requirement by a considerable margin. For cryptographic applications the residual failure probability is negligible.

§6. Relation to other sieves

The Column-Class Sieve may be viewed as a specialization of the classical wheel sieve construction, tuned to the arithmetic form q = kd + 1 and adapted to exploit the mod-6 structure identified in Theorem 7. Generic wheel sieves give residue reductions based on a fixed small modulus; the Column-Class construction extends this by layering additional residue constraints in a particular order, and by taking the grid structure of Theorem 7 as its starting point.

Compared to a generic Eratosthenes sieve applied to the same range, the Column-Class Sieve is measurably faster for NTT-prime generation because it avoids materializing the majority of candidates in the first place.
